## 1. Cognitive Bias Literature

### Daniel Kahneman: System 1 vs System 2

SPAR is fundamentally a **System 2 activation tool**.

Kahneman's research (*Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011) established that human cognition operates in two modes:

- **System 1**: Fast, automatic, intuitive, effortless
- **System 2**: Slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful

Most decisions are made by System 1, which is efficient but prone to systematic biases. SPAR works by:

- Forcing explicit articulation of reasoning (activates System 2)
- Creating cognitive friction through genuine disagreement
- Preventing premature closure on comfortable conclusions

**Key Biases SPAR Addresses:**
| Bias | Description | How SPAR Counters |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Confirmation Bias** | Seeking evidence that supports existing beliefs | Personas actively argue against the leader's lean |
| **Anchoring** | Over-weighting initial information | Multiple personas provide different "anchors" |
| **Availability Heuristic** | Overweighting recent/salient information | Personas bring different information frames |
| **Overconfidence** | Excessive certainty in one's judgments | INTERROGATE step stress-tests synthesis |

### Philip Tetlock: Superforecasting

Tetlock's research (*Superforecasting*, 2015) found that the best predictors:

- **Update beliefs frequently** in response to new evidence
- **Consider multiple perspectives** genuinely
- **Decompose problems** into tractable sub-questions
- **Acknowledge uncertainty** explicitly

SPAR embeds these practices structurally:
- **RUMBLE** forces belief updating through clash
- **Personas** provide genuine perspective diversity
- **SPARKIT protocol** decomposes deliberation into phases
- **Dissent documentation** preserves acknowledged uncertainty

## 2. Group Decision-Making Research

### Irving Janis: Groupthink

Janis's research on foreign policy disasters (*Groupthink*, 1972) identified how cohesive groups suppress dissent:

- Illusion of invulnerability
- Collective rationalization
- Stereotyping of out-groups
- Self-censorship
- Illusion of unanimity
- Pressure on dissenters

**SPAR as Anti-Groupthink:**

| Groupthink Symptom | SPAR Countermeasure |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Self-censorship | Personas are *assigned* to dissent |
| Illusion of unanimity | Synthesis *documents* remaining disagreement |
| Pressure on dissenters | Dissent is structurally *required* |
| Collective rationalization | Multiple frames prevent single narrative |

### Garold Stasser: Hidden Profiles

Stasser's research (1985, 1989) showed that groups systematically fail to share unique information:

- **Shared information** (known to all) dominates discussion
- **Unique information** (known to one member) gets suppressed
- Groups converge on worse decisions because they discuss what everyone already knows

SPAR's **Persona structure** counters this by:
- Assigning personas to *different information frames*
- Requiring articulation of what each persona "sees that others might miss"
- Using the **Absent Voice** (ASPIRES pattern) to surface unrepresented perspectives

## 3. Argumentation Theory

### Hugo Mercier & Dan Sperber: The Argumentative Theory of Reasoning

Mercier & Sperber's research (*The Enigma of Reason*, 2017) proposes that human reasoning evolved not for solitary truth-seeking, but for **argumentation in social contexts**:

> "Reason evolved to help us argue with others, not to discover the truth on our own."

**Implications for SPAR:**

- **Individual reason is biased by design** â€” We're evolved to win arguments, not find truth
- **Reasoning works best in groups** â€” But only when genuine disagreement exists
- **Confirmation bias is a feature, not a bug** â€” It makes us effective advocates for our position
- **Collective reasoning requires structure** â€” Without it, dominant voices win

SPAR creates the **ideal conditions for collective reasoning**:
- Separate reasoners (personas) with committed positions
- Structured exchange of arguments
- Evaluation by neutral synthesis (moderator)
- Documented dissent

### Stephen Toulmin: The Uses of Argument

Toulmin's model (1958) provides the structure of good argument:

- **Claim**: What you're asserting
- **Data**: Evidence supporting the claim
- **Warrant**: Why the data supports the claim
- **Qualifier**: Conditions or limits on the claim
- **Rebuttal**: Exceptions or counter-arguments
- **Backing**: Support for the warrant itself

SPAR's persona prompts embed Toulmin's structure:
- "What's your position?" â€” Claim
- "What evidence supports it?" â€” Data
- "Why does that evidence matter?" â€” Warrant
- "What would change your mind?" â€” Qualifier/Rebuttal

## 4. Dialectical Reasoning

### Hegelian Dialectic

The thesis-antithesis-synthesis model, often attributed to Hegel (though actually Fichte's formulation):

1. **Thesis**: An initial proposition
2. **Antithesis**: A contradicting proposition
3. **Synthesis**: A resolution that transcends both

SPAR extends this with:
- **Multiple theses** (4+ personas, not just 2)
- **Structured clash** (not just logical contradiction)
- **Preserved dissent** (synthesis doesn't erase disagreement)

### Socratic Method

The dialectical approach of Socrates:
- Question assumptions
- Expose contradictions
- Drive toward deeper understanding

SPAR's **INTERROGATE** step (Step 6 of SPARKIT) embodies this:
- Challenge the synthesis
- Probe hidden assumptions
- Surface what remains unexamined

## 5. AI-Specific Research

### The Problem of Coherence Collapse

When AI systems are asked for "multiple perspectives," they tend to:
- Generate perspectives that share underlying assumptions
- Converge toward "balanced" outputs
- Lack genuine epistemic diversity

This happens because:
- Single model = single "world model"
- Training optimizes for coherence
- RLHF rewards "helpful" (often diplomatic) responses

**SPAR addresses this by:**
- Separate persona instantiations with distinct prompts
- Explicit instruction to *not* be balanced
- Moderator synthesis that names tensions rather than resolving them

### Constitutional AI & Role-Playing

Research on AI role-playing (Anthropic, 2023) shows:
- LLMs can maintain distinct "personalities" when properly prompted
- Role fidelity improves with clear character sheets
- Persona consistency degrades without structural reinforcement

SPAR's **persona templates** incorporate these findings:
- Complete character sheets (priority, fear, style, non-negotiable)
- Explicit "don't break character" instructions
- Multi-turn reinforcement of persona identity

## 6. Organizational Development Research

### Edgar Schein: Humble Inquiry

Schein's research (*Humble Inquiry*, 2013) argues that most organizational failures stem from:
- Telling instead of asking
- Assuming instead of inquiring
- Status dynamics suppressing information flow

SPAR institutionalizes inquiry:
- Personas must ask questions, not just assert
- GRACE's **R â€” Reveal** explicitly addresses status dynamics and anxieties
- The methodology values *not knowing* as a starting condition

### Chris Argyris: Defensive Reasoning

Argyris's research on organizational learning shows that professionals:
- Avoid testing their assumptions publicly
- Protect themselves from embarrassment
- Create "undiscussables" that block learning

SPAR makes the undiscussable *discussable*:
- Personas have permission to say uncomfortable things
- The Framework Critic (PROBE) attacks the methodology itself
- ASPIRES patterns like **Pre-Mortem** assume failure to surface fears
